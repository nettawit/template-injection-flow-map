Harmony · Template Injection

Product Gaps & Research Findings

A summary of what's working, what's not, and where we need to focus next.

Wix Research
July 2026
Internal
Research method

How the research was done

Manual testing across multiple templates and business types — in two rounds.

1️⃣
Open a template
Selected a range of Wix templates across different categories — fitness, fashion, marketing, art, SaaS. Each template has its own visual identity and layout.
2️⃣
Round 1 — basic ask
Asked Aria to replace the content and images to fit a specific business type. No additional guidance — just the business description.
3️⃣
Round 2 — with style anchor
Asked again, this time adding: "keep the same style and layout as the template." Compared the two results side by side.
The question we were answering
Does telling Aria to preserve the template's visual style produce a meaningfully better result — and if so, how much better?
Round 1

"Change the content and images to fit [business type]"

The basic ask — no style or layout guidance. Aria personalizes the content, but without an anchor to the template's visual identity.

Key Finding

"…and keep the same style and layout as the template"

Adding one sentence changed the output significantly. When Aria is told to preserve the template's visual style, the result is more coherent, more complete, and feels like it belongs in the template.

Same template — original
Same starting point — same template, same business type asked. The only difference is the instruction. Click to replace with your screenshot.
After injection — style preserved
With the style instruction, Aria stays true to the template's visual identity. Content feels like a natural extension of the design — not a forced overlay.
The instruction that made the difference
"Change the content and images to fit [business type] — and keep the same style and layout as the template."
Gap — still remains

Images in app widgets aren't updated

Even with the style instruction, Wix app widgets (Stores, Gallery, Bookings) are outside Aria's injection scope — their images stay unchanged regardless of what you ask.

Page sections — updated correctly
Aria updated hero text and images: "Bespoke by Nature" — art & crafts content injected into what was a fashion template. The page section looks great.
Store widget — still shows original clothing
Scrolling down — the Wix Stores product grid still shows the original clothing items. Aria can't reach app widget content. The personalized experience breaks here.
Why this matters
App widgets (Gallery, Stores, Bookings) are often the most prominent visual areas on a page. When they keep generic images, the site feels half-personalized — and the mismatch is jarring.
Gap — still remains

Bigger pivots are still hard

The style instruction helps, but when the business type is very different from the template's original purpose, the structure itself becomes a constraint — content can't fully adapt to the wrong container.

Same business type — works well
Gym template injected for a gym business — "REDEFINE YOUR LIMITS". Content, images, and structure all align. The injection feels natural because the business type matches.
Different business type — forced fit
Fashion/clothing store template injected for an art & crafts shop — "Bespoke by Nature". Hero adapts, but the underlying Store structure, product grid, and nav reveal the mismatch.

Gap — still remains

Injection can be incomplete

Even with the style instruction, not every section gets updated consistently. Some elements — like the hero headline — are sometimes skipped while others (like the nav) are updated. The result feels partial.

Original template
Template as-is — every section uses consistent copy tied to the template's purpose. Click to replace with your screenshot.
After injection — partial update
Nav updated to "EPS MARKETING" but the hero headline still reads "Your Brand's Key to Better Marketing" — generic template copy left in place. Partial injection breaks the experience.
The pattern
Aria tends to update some sections confidently (nav, short text blocks) while leaving others untouched (hero headlines, longer body sections). The inconsistency is noticeable to the user.
Summary

What we learned

Core finding
Adding "keep the same style and layout" to the injection prompt produces significantly better results — content feels like it belongs in the template, not forced into it.
Issue What happens Impact Fix
No style instruction Content drifts from template identity; partial updates; visual incoherence High Prompt fix
Incomplete injection Some sections updated (nav), others skipped (hero) — inconsistent experience High Open
Images in app widgets Gallery, Stores, Bookings images unchanged — mismatch with updated sections High Open
Big business type pivots Template structure too rigid for very different business types Medium Open
What's next

Next steps

1
Add character-limit awareness to injection
Pass container max-char limits as part of the injection context so Aria generates text that respects layout constraints.
2
Extend image injection to Wix app widgets
Gallery, Stores, and Bookings widgets need a separate injection path. Coordinate with app teams to expose image update APIs during personalization.
3
Make brand & style preservation a default
Include a brand/style instruction in every injection prompt by default. Already proven to improve output quality — low effort, high impact.
4
Research template–business type matching
Filter or rank templates by business type similarity to reduce the frequency of large pivots before injection even begins.
